Research Context

This study examines how teachers in Nakuru County, Kenya navigate the complexities of inclusive education within the Competency-Based Education (CBE) curriculum. Through in-depth semi-structured interviews with nine teachers across diverse contexts—from urban schools with specialized support to rural schools with extreme resource constraints—this analysis reveals the lived realities of inclusive pedagogy in Kenya's evolving educational landscape.

Research Questions Addressed

Primary Research Question

How do teachers in Nakuru County adjust their teaching practices to support learners with diverse learning needs under the CBE curriculum?

Secondary Questions

  • What learner differences do teachers identify in their classrooms?
  • What pedagogical adaptations and strategies do teachers employ?
  • What resources and support systems are available or lacking?
  • How do teachers measure inclusive success (presence, participation, progression)?
  • What are the personal and professional costs of adaptation?
  • What systemic barriers and enablers exist?

🔍 Key Findings

Finding 1: The Resourcefulness-Resource Gap Paradox

The Challenge

Teachers demonstrate extraordinary resourcefulness and creativity in adapting instruction for diverse learners, yet this resourcefulness exists because of—not despite—severe resource constraints. Teachers are personalizing costs that should be systemic investments.

  • 100% of teachers reported using personal phones and data bundles
  • KICD provides links but "zero resources" (T01)
  • Teachers create materials, print at own cost, improvise constantly
  • Rural-urban technology divide is stark

Implication

While teacher agency should be celebrated, relying on individual resourcefulness creates inequitable outcomes. Urban teachers with better personal resources can do more; rural teachers cannot. Systemic resource provision is essential for equitable inclusion.

Finding 2: Co-Teaching as a Game-Changer for Inclusion

The advantage part of it, my co-teacher is an SNE teacher.
— T09, Urban, Grade 1

The 4 teachers with co-teachers (T02, T06, T07, T09) consistently demonstrated:

  • Greater confidence in individualizing instruction
  • More sophisticated grouping strategies
  • Ability to provide one-on-one support without neglecting whole class
  • Reduced teacher stress and burden

Recommendation

Expand co-teaching models nationally, particularly pairing general education teachers with SNE specialists. This model aligns with CBE's learner-centered philosophy and provides immediate, in-class support.

Finding 3: Translanguaging as Subversive Inclusion

100% of teachers reported using Kiswahili or mother tongue to support comprehension, despite knowing learners are penalized for not using English.

That one I only use for that specific boy. And I don't actually do it openly sometimes. Sometimes I just call him aside, maybe in the staffroom, and guide him.
— T03, Rural, Grade 6

The Contradiction

Policy penalizes multilingualism, but teachers recognize it as essential for access. Teachers are operating in a zone of pedagogical disobedience to be inclusive.

Recommendation

Legitimize and resource translanguaging practices. Develop official guidelines that permit strategic use of home languages and Kiswahili for scaffolding, especially for learners with language-based learning barriers.

Finding 4: The Dropout Data Discrepancy

Most teachers reported "100% attendance" when asked initially, but probing revealed:

  • T01: "Around 2% dropped" from Grade 9 due to CBE being "too heavy"
  • T08: Sporadic attendance tied to farm labor cycles; students "sneak through the fence"
  • T07: Students come "only on Fridays for announcements"
That's good to hear because I've been hearing a different story from the others. People are telling me attendance has been 100%, everything is working great.
— Researcher
No, no, no.
— T01, correcting the narrative

The Challenge

There appears to be social desirability bias in reporting, and/or administrative pressure to show success. Presence is being overstated, masking real exclusion.

Finding 5: "Small Wins" as Valid Progression Metrics

Teachers in this study rejected deficit framing and celebrated incremental progress:

As a teacher for special needs, if they count to three, that's a step. Then from there, you just continue slowly.
— T04, SNE School
This child was not able to read a three letter word. Now he can be able to do it. But in Grade 1, you're learning about single sentences. It is a step. We are still getting somewhere.
— T09, Urban, Grade 1

Teachers look for:

  • Observable behavior change ("the way they interact")
  • Confidence building (raising hand even if answer is wrong)
  • Skill acquisition (copying one word instead of none)
  • Non-academic competencies (following instructions, socializing)

Implication

Formalize "small wins" within assessment frameworks. CBE's competency focus creates space for this, but it must be explicit in policy to counter the persistent exam-pressure culture.

Finding 6: The Parent Education Gap

8/9 teachers identified parent misunderstanding of CBE—particularly pathways—as a major barrier to inclusive outcomes.

Some parents who did not understand the pathways, the children themselves and the parents, they just chose schools without knowing about the pathways. Now transitioning to senior school was a big issue.
— T09, Urban

Only T09 described a successful parent education model from her daughter's private school: a facilitator-led session for Grade 6 and 9 parents explaining pathways, tracking performance from Grade 6, and aligning aspirations with aptitudes.

Recommendation

Implement structured, facilitator-led parent education programs starting in Grade 6, not Grade 9. Cover: pathways, competency vs. grades, career alignment, how to support learning at home. Make this a national policy requirement, not optional.

Finding 7: The 20% Retooling Crisis

Multiple teachers estimated that only ~20% of teachers have received adequate CBE training, 10 years into implementation.

My honest opinion is that on the retooling side, the government has managed to do around 20%. But imagine there are some teachers who are teaching the new system without proper training.
— T01, Rural

The Challenge

Teachers are teaching subjects they were not trained in, using methods they were not trained in, serving a curriculum they don't fully understand. This fundamentally undermines inclusion.

Urgent Recommendation

Declare a national CBE retooling emergency. Mandate and fund universal teacher training within 2 years. Prioritize rural schools and teachers teaching outside their specialization.

🌍 Contextual Analysis

Comparison: Urban vs. Rural vs. SNE School Experiences

Urban Schools (T06, T09, T04)

  • Strengths: Technology access, SNE units, co-teaching models, parental support, resource availability
  • Challenges: Still face time pressure, zonal assessments, need for specialized materials
  • Inclusion approach: Structured, multi-tiered support systems; pull-out services combined with in-class differentiation
  • Quote: "In my school, it's okay. And the learners also are quite good with ICT. They are used to tablets." (T09)

Rural Schools (T01, T03, T05, T08)

  • Strengths: Teacher dedication, peer support networks, improvisation skills
  • Challenges: Extreme resource scarcity, teacher shortages, parental resistance, generational poverty/illiteracy, hunger, child labor
  • Inclusion approach: Highly individualized, teacher-dependent; relies on teacher resourcefulness
  • Quote: "You find that in that school there is no electricity. You have the tablets, but you cannot be able to use them." (T09, reflecting on previous rural post)

Special Education School (T04)

  • Strengths: Specialized training, adapted curriculum, flexible timelines, IEP frameworks, therapeutic support
  • Challenges: Only 2 assistive devices for 31 learners, dependent on head teacher support, still using personal resources
  • Inclusion approach: Fully individualized, stage-based (foundational, intermediate, pre-vocational); focus on ADLs and life skills
  • Quote: "When teaching learners with special needs, it comes from your heart. If you don't have humanity in your heart, even if you've trained in special needs, you cannot teach." (T04)

💡 Evidence-Based Recommendations

For Policy Makers (National Level)

1. Declare a CBE Retooling Emergency

Evidence: 80% of teachers inadequately trained (per participant estimates)

Action: Mandate universal retraining within 24 months; prioritize rural and multi-subject teachers; make retooling completion a requirement for continued employment.

2. Legitimize and Resource Translanguaging

Evidence: 100% of teachers use translanguaging; it's currently "subversive"

Action: Develop official translanguaging guidelines; provide multilingual learning materials; train teachers in strategic code-switching for scaffolding.

3. Implement National Co-Teaching Initiative

Evidence: Co-teaching dramatically improves inclusion outcomes

Action: Pilot co-teaching in 50 schools per county; pair general ed with SNE teachers; evaluate and scale within 3 years.

4. Establish Rural School Equity Fund

Evidence: Systematic rural disadvantage across all metrics

Action: Ring-fenced budget for: solar technology kits, mobile resource libraries, rural teacher salary incentives (+30%), feeding programs.

5. Mandate Parent Education Programs

Evidence: Parent confusion undermining CBE, especially pathways

Action: Structured sessions starting Grade 6; facilitator-led; cover pathways, competencies, home support strategies. Make attendance linked to school enrollment.

For County Education Officials (Nakuru & Beyond)

6. Create County-Wide Resource-Sharing Platforms

Evidence: Teachers rely on informal WhatsApp groups

Action: Formalize digital resource repositories; curate lesson plans, videos, worksheets by grade/subject; provide download access (not view-only).

7. Establish Mobile SNE Support Teams

Evidence: Rural schools lack SNE specialists

Action: County-funded teams that circuit to rural schools monthly; provide assessments, IEP development support, teacher consultation.

8. Subsidize Teacher Data Costs

Evidence: 100% of teachers use personal phones/bundles

Action: County-negotiated bulk data plans for teachers; or monthly stipend of KES 1,000-2,000 for professional internet use.

For School Leadership

9. Protect Teacher Planning Time

Evidence: Adaptation is time-intensive; lunch hours sacrificed

Action: Timetable-embedded planning periods; reduce teacher load where teaching outside specialization; collaborative planning time for co-teachers.

10. Formalize Peer Collaboration Structures

Evidence: Subject panels and informal networks are lifelines

Action: Monthly protected time for subject panels; cross-curricular meetings (e.g., STEM + language teachers); document and share best practices.

For Teacher Educators & Professional Development

11. Embed Inclusive Pedagogy in Pre-Service Training

Evidence: Teachers learning "on the job" through trial and error

Action: Mandatory inclusive education modules in all teacher training programs; practicum placements must include diverse learner contexts.

12. Create "Small Wins" Assessment Toolkits

Evidence: Teachers value incremental progress but lack formal frameworks

Action: Develop and disseminate progression monitoring tools that capture micro-competencies; train teachers in formative assessment for diverse learners.

For Teachers (Peer-to-Peer Recommendations)

13. Document Your Adaptations

Teachers in this study demonstrated brilliance but it remains tacit knowledge. Create simple logs of what works for which learners; share in WhatsApp groups; build collective expertise.

14. Advocate for Your Needs

Your resourcefulness is admirable but shouldn't be required. Collectively articulate needs to administration/TSC/county officials. Use data: "I spent KES X on bundles this term; we need school WiFi."

📋 Analysis Methodology

Analytical Approach

This analysis employed a hybrid thematic analysis approach combining:

  • Deductive coding: Guided by research questions and the "3 Ps" framework (Presence, Participation, Progression)
  • Inductive coding: Allowing emergent themes from data (e.g., translanguaging as subversion, rural-urban divide)
  • Constant comparative method: Comparing incidents, codes, and categories across transcripts

Coding Process

Phase 1: Familiarization (reading all 9 transcripts multiple times)
Phase 2: Open coding (line-by-line coding, generating initial codes)
Phase 3: Axial coding (grouping codes into categories)
Phase 4: Selective coding (identifying core themes and relationships)
Phase 5: Theme refinement and validation (checking against all transcripts)
Phase 6: Cross-case analysis (comparing across contexts, grades, experience)

Data Management

  • Dataset: 9 semi-structured interviews; ~150,000 words
  • Software: Manual coding supplemented by AI-assisted pattern recognition
  • Coding units: Segments (phrases to paragraphs) carrying complete ideas
  • Inter-coder reliability: Single coder with systematic re-checking

Trustworthiness Measures

Criterion Strategy Employed
Credibility Prolonged engagement with data; participant quotes as evidence; triangulation across 9 diverse contexts
Transferability Thick description; detailed participant profiles; context documentation
Dependability Audit trail; systematic coding process; transparency in methodology
Confirmability Direct quotes anchor all claims; coding hierarchy made explicit; alternative interpretations considered

Limitations

Acknowledged Limitations

  • Sampling: Purposive sample from Nakuru County; may not represent all of Kenya
  • Social desirability bias: Teachers may overstate success; attendance data discrepancy suggests this
  • Researcher positionality: Researcher is fellow teacher; may create rapport but also bias
  • Single point in time: Snapshot during one academic year; no longitudinal perspective
  • Missing voices: No student, parent, or administrator perspectives in this dataset

Ethical Considerations

  • Informed consent: Verbal consent obtained from all participants
  • Anonymization: All identifying details removed (names, schools)
  • Secure storage: Password-protected, university-compliant storage
  • Right to withdraw: Participants informed of voluntary nature
  • Reciprocity: Findings to be shared with participants
